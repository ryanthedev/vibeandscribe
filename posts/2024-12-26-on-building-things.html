On Building Things That Last

There's something to be said for craftsmanship in an age of disposability. When we build with intention, when we choose materials—physical or digital—that age gracefully, we're making a statement about what we value.

I've been thinking about this as I built this blog. The choice to use plain HTML and CSS, no build step, no framework. It's not the modern way. But there's something appealing about a website that will work in twenty years, in any browser, with no dependencies to break or security patches to apply.

The best time to plant a tree was twenty years ago. The second best time is now.

When I look at old furniture—a walnut desk from the 1940s, a leather chair worn soft by decades of use—I see objects that have outlasted trends, outlasted their makers, outlasted whole eras of design philosophy. They persist because they were built with care, from materials that develop character rather than decay.

Digital Patina

Can digital things have patina? I think they can, in their own way. A personal website that's been updated over years, accumulating posts and memories. A piece of software that's been refined through thousands of small improvements. A codebase that shows the marks of many hands, many decisions, many lessons learned.

The opposite of patina is churn—the constant rewriting, the framework du jour, the assumption that whatever exists must be replaced. Sometimes replacement is necessary. Often it's just fashion.

What Lasts

Text lasts. HTML lasts. Simple things last. Complexity decays.

I'm not arguing for technological stasis. New tools solve real problems. But there's wisdom in asking, before adopting something new: will this still work in five years? In ten? Will anyone understand it? Will I?

The web has a wonderful property: backward compatibility. A webpage from 1995 still renders. The same cannot be said for most software ecosystems. Perhaps there's something to learn from that.

Building Slow

I've started approaching projects differently. Instead of reaching for the most powerful tool, I reach for the simplest one that will do the job. Instead of optimizing for development speed, I optimize for comprehensibility.

This isn't always possible. Some problems genuinely require complex solutions. But many don't. Many problems have been solved before, simply, and those solutions still work.

There's a pleasure in building things that feel solid. Things with weight. Things you could hand to someone and say: here, this is yours now—it will serve you well.

That's what I'm aiming for, anyway. One page at a time.
